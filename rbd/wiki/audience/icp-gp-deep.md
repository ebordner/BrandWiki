# ICP — GP deep profile

*Fly-on-the-wall internal dialogue and buying psychology of the VC General Partner ICP. Use for Salesumentary scenes, ad copy, content hooks, and GP outreach. For top-level overview, see [ICP Profile — Primary](icp-primary.md).*

---

## A fly on the wall — his actual internal dialogue

### Monday morning — portfolio update emails

He scans updates.

- Company A: "Strong user growth."
- Company B: "Exciting AI roadmap."
- Company C: "Revenue slightly below projection but strong engagement."

He leans back. His thought: **"Okay… but where's the leverage?"**

Not: "Where's the vision?" He's looking for: margin improvement, revenue per customer expansion, structural tightening. He rarely sees it clearly.

### Investment committee prep

He's reviewing a potential follow-on check. The founder is asking for more capital to "accelerate AI integration."

He thinks: **"Are we accelerating advantage or accelerating burn?"**

He pulls up financials. Revenue is growing — but slowly. Costs are rising — quickly.

He mutters to himself: *"This doesn't feel tight."*

### In the partner meeting

One partner says: "We don't want to miss this wave."

He nods. But inside: **"I don't want to overpay for noise."**

He's thinking: *"Can this business stand on its own without the AI narrative?"* If the answer is no — that's fragile.

### The board meeting

He asks the founder: "How does this improve retention?"

Founder answers with feature explanation. He presses: "How does this improve gross margin?"

Founder pivots to roadmap. He feels it: **"They don't actually know."**

That's the moment his trust softens.

### The AI compression reality

He knows something founders don't fully grasp yet: AI is lowering barriers everywhere. What used to be a 2-year build advantage is now a 6-month sprint.

He thinks: **"Distribution and structure matter more than features now."**

But when he looks at portfolio companies, he sees: feature velocity, hiring velocity, burn velocity. He doesn't always see: revenue architecture discipline.

### The quiet stress

He has LP calls coming up. LPs ask: "How are you positioned in AI?"

He gives a confident answer. But internally he's calculating:
- How many companies are truly defensible?
- How many are narrative-heavy?
- How many depend on being first?
- How many collapse if a platform shifts?

**He doesn't want surprises.**

### The AI panic moment — full scene

He's reading TechCrunch. Another AI-native startup just raised at a massive valuation. One of his portfolio companies is in the same space. His phone buzzes. Partner message: "Have you seen this?"

*His first thought:* **"Are we about to get leapfrogged?"** Not: "This is exciting." Not: "This validates the market." Just: "Are we exposed?"

At the investment committee, a founder presents their AI integration. It demos well. Looks impressive. He nods.

*But he's thinking:*
- "Is this defensible?"
- "Is this monetizable?"
- "Or is this just catching up?"

**The real fear:** He's not afraid of AI. He's afraid of: portfolio irrelevance, overvaluation built on hype, moats collapsing overnight, distribution advantages evaporating, Labs killing companies in one release cycle.

*He thinks:* **"If this can be replicated in 90 days, what did we actually fund?"**

**The silent calculation:** He scans his portfolio mentally. Who's exposed? Who's feature-heavy and structure-light? Who's monetization is thin? Who's dependent on narrative?

He doesn't say it out loud. But the thought is clear: *"How many of these are built to last in an AI cycle?"*

## His specific micro frustrations

Not abstract. Specific:

- Founders confusing product updates with structural progress
- Revenue decks that don't tie features to margin
- No clear mapping between product decisions and enterprise value
- Roadmaps that shift quarterly
- Portfolio dashboards full of activity but light on durability
- AI being used as a story, not a strategy
- Having to ask the same monetization questions repeatedly
- Feeling like he's reacting to hype cycles instead of getting ahead of risk

**He hates reactive posture.**

His most painful micro problems (in his exact words):
1. "They're building fast but I don't know if it's monetizable."
2. "The numbers look good but I don't know if they're durable."
3. "They keep adding features. I don't know what actually drives revenue."
4. "Their forecast works… if everything goes right."
5. "I don't want to pour more capital into something loose."
6. "We might be scaling something fragile."
7. "I can't tell which portfolio companies are structurally strong versus just riding momentum."

**The most painful one:** *"What if this fails and we could have seen it earlier?"*

He can tolerate risk. **He hates avoidable collapse.**

## What he actually wants (in his exact words)

- "Show me how this makes money — cleanly."
- "Show me why this holds at 10x."
- "Show me what kills this."
- "Show me the structural risk before it becomes visible."
- "Show me why we won't get blindsided."
- "Show me how this increases defensibility."
- "Show me how this strengthens margin."
- "Show me how this ties to enterprise value."

He wants discipline. He wants visibility. He wants founders who understand that **capital is leverage — not permission.**

## The three buying triggers

### Safety trigger

He does not feel safe because someone promises growth. He feels safe when:
- He can see structural clarity across a portfolio company
- Someone can articulate what's weak without being dramatic
- Revenue assumptions are pressure-tested
- Product and financial logic are tied together
- He feels ahead of risk, not behind it

**His safety trigger is control.** Not hype. Not optimism. Not more reporting. Control.

He buys when he thinks: *"This gives me visibility I don't currently have."* Or: *"This helps me avoid a preventable mistake."*

### Belonging trigger

He sits in rooms with other GPs, LPs, sophisticated operators. He wants to feel: disciplined, strategic, not emotional, not reactive.

He feels belonging when:
- He sounds measured
- His decisions feel structured
- He can explain why a company is strong or weak
- He is not relying on founder charisma

He belongs in rooms where capital is allocated intentionally. He does not want to look like he is betting. He wants to look like he is **underwriting.**

He feels belonging when he can say: *"We've stress-tested this."*

### Identity trigger

He does not want to be: the GP who funded hype, the GP who missed structural cracks, the GP who had preventable losses, the GP who was late to see monetization weakness.

He wants to be: the disciplined allocator, the GP who sees structural strength early, the GP who backs companies that scale cleanly, the one whose portfolio companies dominate.

His identity is not "visionary." It's **sharp. Measured. Disciplined. Unemotional about capital.**

He wants to feel: *"I back durable companies."*

## The highest-value micro experiences he desires

Not dashboards. Not more reporting. He wants:
- A clean lens into structural strength
- Clarity on which revenue models are real
- Confidence before follow-on capital
- Fewer surprises
- Cleaner board conversations
- The ability to call out drift early

He wants to look at a company and think: **"This one is tight."**

*That word matters to him. Tight.*

---

## What actually makes him buy

He buys when:
- A portfolio company feels loose
- A founder sounds confident but vague
- Growth is happening but monetization clarity is thin
- A follow-on round decision feels risky
- He senses drift but can't quantify it

He buys when he thinks: *"I need someone who can look under the hood and tell me if this is structurally sound."*

He does not buy because he wants help. He buys because he wants **certainty.**

---

## His internal buying equation

> When I eliminate structural weaknesses in a portfolio company before scaling further,
> Then I can deploy capital with confidence instead of instinct,
> And I become the GP known for backing durable, dominant companies.

Or in plainer language:

> When I know which companies are actually solid,
> Then I stop worrying about avoidable collapse,
> And I am the disciplined investor I claim to be.

## The aircraft analogy (for content and Salesumentary)

He runs a fleet, not a single car. He has 20 engines running. Some sound good. Some look promising. Some are loud and impressive. But he cannot disassemble all of them himself. He's relying on: founder updates, financial projections, narrative confidence, growth charts.

*It's like investing in aircraft mid-flight. One of those planes has a hairline crack in the wing. It's invisible at cruising altitude. It only fails under turbulence.*

That's what structural revenue weakness looks like. It does not show up in good months. It shows up under stress — down market, rising CAC, churn spike, slower fundraising environment.

**Most VCs operate like weather watchers.** They look at the forecast. **RbD operates like a structural inspector.** Checks the foundation before expansion.

## The unique insight RbD brings (in his framing)

Startups do not fail because they are bad. They fail because small structural weaknesses compound. Like compound interest — but in reverse.

A slightly wrong monetization assumption + a slightly bloated roadmap + a slightly optimistic retention model + a slightly reactive expansion strategy = over 24 months, collapse.

RbD interrupts compounding fragility. And replaces it with compounding leverage. That is not growth consulting. **That is architecture.**
