# ICP profile — primary

RbD serves two distinct primary buyers who share one underlying conviction: structure determines survival.

---

## ICP 1: The Venture-Backed Founder

### Who they are

- Pre-Series A founders struggling with traction
- Series A founders under board scrutiny
- Scaling founders experiencing rework loops, stalled growth, tech misalignment
- Building in SaaS, AI, fintech, or adjacent tech categories
- Company size: 11–500 employees
- Has traction, has some revenue, but architecture hasn't been stress-tested

### The identity gap

**She thinks she is:** A high-performing founder scaling a breakout company. "I've raised capital. We have traction. We're moving."

**She is actually:** At an inflection point where architecture matters more than ambition. Operating a company that looks strong — but hasn't been fully engineered for altitude. Some decisions were tactical. Some were reactive. Some were never engineered.

**She wants to become:** A CEO whose company holds at unicorn scale. A founder whose revenue model compounds under pressure. A leader who walks into every board meeting with calm structural confidence.

**What's blocking her:**
- Product decisions not tethered to monetization logic
- Revenue expansion pathways not intentionally architected
- Technical infrastructure layered under urgency
- Scale amplifying complexity instead of leverage
- AI accelerating speed without structural coherence

### Her micro problems (in her exact language)

Daily symptoms:
- "We keep rebuilding features."
- "Investors want traction, but revenue is inconsistent."
- "I can't clearly explain how our pricing ties to margin."
- "Our backend and our promise don't match."
- "The CRM doesn't give me lifecycle visibility."
- "We're growing, but I don't trust the foundation."

AI-specific tension:
- "A competitor could rebuild this in 60 days."
- "Is our moat real?"
- "Are we scaling something fragile?"

### Her micro desires (what she actually wants)

- Calm before board meetings
- Clear revenue logic tied to product specs
- Clean pricing-to-margin architecture
- Tech stack mapped to lifecycle
- Service design blueprint across Learn → Buy → Get → Use → Pay → Support
- Confidence scaling

### The identity gap in one sentence

*She is scaling momentum when she needs to be scaling architecture.*

### Her internal buying trigger

She buys when she sees a concrete mapping — Feature → Revenue → Margin → Enterprise Value — applied to *her* company. Not theory. Her product. Her pricing. Her architecture. The moment she recognizes: "This is why we're spinning." "This is why that feature didn't convert."

### Refusal hooks (language that lands)

- "For the founder who refuses to scale a revenue model they haven't stress-tested."
- "If you're the kind of founder who won't let growth hide structural weakness."
- "This is for the founder who is done assuming momentum equals durability."
- "For the founder who refuses to let valuation outpace monetization logic."
- "For the founder who refuses to raise another round without knowing the architecture holds."
- "This is for the founder who is done shipping features that don't expand revenue."
- "If you're the kind of founder who won't let AI accelerate misalignment inside your company."

## ICP 2: The Venture Capital GP

### Who they are

- GP at a venture fund, Series A/B focus
- Managing a portfolio of funded companies
- Concerned about overvaluation
- Concerned about AI compressing moats
- Concerned about underwriting fragility disguised as traction
- Wanting durable portfolio construction

### The identity gap

**He thinks he is:** A disciplined capital allocator backing exceptional founders. "I select well. I support well. I allocate strategically."

**He is actually:** Managing structural risk that lives inside his portfolio companies. He can pick strong founders — but cannot guarantee revenue architecture discipline post-investment.

**He wants to become:** A GP known for portfolio durability. A fund with fewer stalls, fewer emergency restructures, stronger exits. A capital steward whose companies scale cleanly.

**What's blocking him:**
- Portfolio companies scaling before monetization alignment
- Follow-on capital deployed before structural stress-testing
- Revenue logic validated too late
- AI layered onto messy systems
- Growth metrics masking architectural fragility

### His micro problems (in his exact language)

- "Revenue without defensibility."
- "AI compressing time-to-irrelevance."
- "Portfolio companies revising product repeatedly."
- "Margin inconsistency across investments."
- "Distribution dependence risk."
- "Labs could wipe this out overnight."

### What he wants

- Visibility
- Structural underwriting
- Durable revenue logic
- Board-ready clarity
- Predictable margin modeling
- Defensibility visibility
- Portfolio durability narrative

### The identity gap in one sentence

*He is managing structural risk he cannot see inside his portfolio companies.*

### His internal buying trigger

He leans in when someone shows him — on a *specific company* — exactly where the structural risks are. Clean answers. Not narratives. Map of exposure + reinforcement roadmap.

### Refusal hooks (language that lands)

- "For the VC GP who refuses to deploy follow-on capital into structural guesswork."
- "If you're the kind of VC GP who won't confuse top-line growth with durable revenue architecture."
- "This is for the VC GP who is done underwriting momentum that hasn't been stress-tested."
- "For the VC GP who refuses to let portfolio valuation compress because monetization wasn't engineered early enough."
- "If you're the kind of VC GP who won't accept 'we'll fix it post-Series A' as a strategy."

## Shared conviction

Both ICPs believe: **structure determines who survives.** The Top 5% don't scale faster. They scale cleaner.

## Disqualifiers

- Founders chasing valuation optics
- Funds underwriting hype cycles
- Anyone who believes speed alone wins
- Anyone pre-revenue or very early stage (may lack urgency or budget for high-ticket)
