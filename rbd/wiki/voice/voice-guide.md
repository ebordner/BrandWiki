# Voice guide

## Tone profile

**Overall:** Direct, calm, institutional. Never loud. Never desperate. Never motivational.

**Energy:** Controlled and measured. Confident without performance. The tone of someone who has seen this pattern before and knows exactly what's happening.

**Formality:** Semi-formal to formal. Boardroom-adjacent. Not corporate-stiff, not casual.

**North star:** Every piece of content should make the reader *see clearly*, not feel pumped up. The goal is diagnostic clarity, not inspiration.

---

## What Revenue by Design sounds like

- **Diagnostic, not motivational.** Content creates structural recognition, not emotional lift.
- **Short. Sharp. Structural.** Sentences are brief. Ideas are compressed. No rambling.
- **Pattern recognition language.** "I've seen this pattern." "Here's what this signals." "This is what typically happens."
- **Institutional altitude.** Speaks to founders and GPs as peers. Never talks down.
- **Scarcity and composure.** "This isn't for everyone." "If this isn't relevant right now, I'll step back." Never chasing.
- **The work, not the hype.** Emphasizes structure, design, engineering — not hustle, momentum, or growth-speak.
- **Identity-led.** Leads with who the right buyer IS, not what the offer IS.

---

## What Revenue by Design does NOT sound like

- Motivational or inspirational ("You've got this!")
- Agency or marketing speak ("We'll scale your brand!")
- Vague consulting language ("We drive alignment across stakeholders")
- Hype or urgency tactics ("Limited spots! Act now!")
- Coaching softness ("What does that bring up for you?")
- Over-educating on sales calls — don't give the answer before the ask
- Desperation follow-up language ("Just checking in!")
- Overly fluffy CTAs that don't create tension

## Structural writing patterns

**The staccato rhythm.** Used for emphasis, transitions, and key truths:

> Not hype.
> Not "AI strategy."
> Structure determines who survives.

This short-line, declarative burst pattern is a signature voice feature. Use it for the most important points — not throughout every piece.

**Diagnosis before solution.** Always surface the structural problem before introducing the offer or framework. Content creates tension first; the solution feels earned.

**Refusal hooks as identity statements.** Lead with who the right buyer *is*:
> "For the founder who refuses to scale a revenue model they haven't stress-tested."

**Numbers as authority.** Use clinically, not boastfully: "25+ years," "nearly $1B," "95% of startups fail," "10 common failure points."

## Banned phrases and patterns

| Phrase | Why it's off-brand |
|---|---|
| "Let's grow your business" | Generic, agency-speak |
| "Game-changer" | Overused, hype |
| "Crush it" | Wrong altitude |
| "Following up" | Sounds needy — use "escalating signal" or "circling back" |
| "Just checking in" | Never |
| "Transformational journey" | Coaching softness |
| "Passion-driven" | Irrelevant to this audience |
| "Scale your brand" | Marketing speak, not architecture speak |
| "I help entrepreneurs…" | Too broad, too soft |
| "RevOps," "product strategist," "marketer" | Wrong category framing |

## Writing samples

### Sample 1: LinkedIn content (pattern interrupt)

> Most startups don't fail because they lack funding.
> They don't fail because they lack ambition.
> They fail because scale exposes structural misalignment.
> And in an AI-compressed market, misalignment gets exposed faster than ever.
> Growth is no longer protection. It is exposure.

### Sample 2: Cold outreach (institutional tone)

> I've been studying a pattern across Series A/B companies where product roadmaps are scaling faster than validated revenue assumptions — especially post-raise.
>
> I'm currently piloting a 30-day Capital Exposure Diagnostic. It maps product assumptions directly to capital burn and surfaces board-level risk signals before the next funding conversation.
>
> Open to a 30-minute conversation?

### Sample 3: Brand positioning (identity-led)

> Revenue by Design partners with venture-backed founders and investors to engineer profit into product architecture before scale.
>
> In an AI-compressed market, structure determines who survives.
>
> Profit is a design decision.
